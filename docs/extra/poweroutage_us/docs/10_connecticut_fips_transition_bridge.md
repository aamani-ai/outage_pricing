# Connecticut FIPS Transition Bridge

Status: implementation note for review
Last updated: 2026-06-19

## 1. Short answer

Connecticut is a county-FIPS continuity problem, not a total outage-data loss
problem.

The official Census geography changed from 8 legacy counties (`09001`-`09015`)
to 9 planning-region county equivalents (`09110`-`09190`). In our EAGLE-I raw
data, the practical source switch appears in 2025: legacy county rows stop around
late May / early June 2025, and planning-region rows start on May 29, 2025.

That means:

- Greenwich can still be priced, but it needs the CT bridge:
  legacy Fairfield County (`09001`) -> Western Connecticut Planning Region
  (`09190`).
- Bridgeport can still be priced, but it maps differently:
  legacy Fairfield County (`09001`) -> Greater Bridgeport Planning Region
  (`09120`).
- A legacy county is not always one new region. Fairfield is the cleanest example:
  it splits across Western Connecticut, Greater Bridgeport, and Naugatuck Valley
  in the town/customer bridge.
- The current pipeline should not treat new `091xx` planning-region rows as if
  they have an 11-year county history. They are a post-switch segment unless we
  explicitly stitch them.

## 2. Why this exists

Most of the outage pricing pipeline is county keyed. Connecticut changed the
meaning of "county" for modern federal geographies, and different data sources
adopted the change in different years.

For our purposes there are three separate questions:

1. What did Census define as current CT county-equivalents?
2. When did each external data source start using those codes?
3. How do we bridge old county keyed outage history to new planning-region
   geography without pretending the mapping is one-to-one?

This note answers the third question at a practical level and records the source
timing we observed in EAGLE-I.

## 3. Official timing versus our source timing

| Source | What changed | Timing relevant to us |
|---|---|---|
| Census / Federal Register | Census adopted CT planning regions as county-equivalent units, replacing the 8 legacy counties for current and future Census products. | Internally in 2022; public data/geospatial products beginning late 2022; by 2024 Census operations/publications use the 9 new county-equivalents except older legacy products. |
| BLS QCEW | QCEW moved to the new CT county definitions. BLS explicitly warns there is no direct old-to-new county mapping. | QCEW uses new CT counties from 2024 forward; old counties before 2024. |
| EAGLE-I raw outage data | Observed CT FIPS behavior switches from legacy county FIPS to planning-region FIPS. | Legacy `090xx` rows stop around 2025-05-29 to 2025-06-02; new `091xx` rows start on 2025-05-29. |

Official references:

- Federal Register: <https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2022/06/06/2022-12063/change-to-county-equivalents-in-the-state-of-connecticut>
- BLS QCEW CT county-equivalent note: <https://www.bls.gov/cew/classifications/areas/new-2024-connecticut-counties.htm>
- Census ACS user note: <https://www.census.gov/programs-surveys/acs/technical-documentation/user-notes/2023-01.html>

## 5. Bridge construction

The bridge is built from two local inputs:

1. PoUS CT town/city feature table with legacy county labels and tracked
   customers:
   [`town_density_features.csv`](../../location_features/analysis/outputs/town_density_features.csv)
2. Census 2023 CT county-subdivision Gazetteer with planning-region FIPS:
   [`2023_gaz_cousubs_09.txt`](../../location_features/data/raw/census_gazetteer/2023_gaz_cousubs_09.txt)

The two streams are joined at town/city name grain:

```text
PoUS pilot rows                         Census 2023 gazetteer
legacy county + tracked customers       planning-region FIPS + town geometry
        |                                        |
        | normalize town / city / borough names  |
        +-------------------+--------------------+
                            |
                            v
        town-level bridge: legacy county <-> planning region
                            |
                            v
        aggregate to old/new overlap weights
```

Artifacts:

- [`ct_legacy_county_planning_region_town_crosswalk.csv`](../analysis/outputs/ct_legacy_county_planning_region_town_crosswalk.csv)
- [`ct_legacy_county_planning_region_overlap_summary.csv`](../analysis/outputs/ct_legacy_county_planning_region_overlap_summary.csv)

Known join exceptions:

- `Hartland` was labeled `Unknown` in the pilot feature table; corrected to
  legacy Hartford County.
- `Southport` appears as a PoUS city/cell but is not a Census municipality; it is
  bridged through Fairfield town.
- `Bozrah` is a Census municipality in legacy New London County but is absent
  from the PoUS pilot feature table. It is retained as `gazetteer_only`.

## 6. What the bridge says

The bridge confirms that old county and new planning-region boundaries cross.
There is no single lookup table like `09001 -> one 091xx`.

Example: legacy Fairfield County (`09001`) splits into:

| Legacy county | Planning region | PoUS tracked customer weight | Share of legacy county weight |
|---|---|---:|---:|
| Fairfield (`09001`) | Western Connecticut (`09190`) | 249,028.0 | 62.2% |
| Fairfield (`09001`) | Greater Bridgeport (`09120`) | 133,133.0 | 33.2% |
| Fairfield (`09001`) | Naugatuck Valley (`09140`) | 18,447.5 | 4.6% |

Two address examples:

| Location | Legacy v0 pricing county | New planning-region county-equivalent |
|---|---|---|
| Greenwich | Fairfield (`09001`) | Western Connecticut (`09190`) |
| Bridgeport | Fairfield (`09001`) | Greater Bridgeport (`09120`) |

So the pricing issue is not "we cannot price Greenwich or Bridgeport." We can,
but we need to be explicit about which geography the price is using:

- v0 county pricing history: legacy county keyed.
- modern Census/Gazetteer geography: planning-region keyed.
- bridge layer: town/city allocation between those two systems.

## 7. How to use this in the pipeline

### 7.1 Quote-time address lookup

For a CT address or lat/lon:

1. Resolve the point to town/census geography.
2. Resolve the town to both:
   - legacy county FIPS, for v0 county pricing continuity;
   - planning-region FIPS, for modern geography display and future data joins.
3. Price from the legacy county until the full CT time-series stitch is approved.
4. Show a CT transition flag in review/debug views so the user knows the county
   and modern planning region differ.

### 7.2 Historical time-series continuity

Do not concatenate raw `090xx` and `091xx` rows by name. The boundaries are
different.

A candidate bridge from legacy counties to planning regions is:

```text
planning_region_history[t]
  = sum over legacy counties:
      legacy_county_history[t]
      * share_of_legacy_pous_weight_to_that_planning_region
```

A candidate bridge from new planning regions back to legacy counties is:

```text
legacy_county_post_switch[t]
  = sum over planning regions:
      planning_region_observed[t]
      * share_of_planning_region_pous_weight_from_that_legacy_county
```

These are bridge estimates, not official observations. They should be tagged as
stitched or approximate, and they should not overwrite raw source values.

### 7.3 Dashboard and model guardrails

- Do not calculate 11-year trends for `091xx` rows unless a stitched planning
  region history exists.
- Do not use the 2025 `091xx` segment as a full-year history without accounting
  for the May 29 start.
- If CT is shown in county trend maps, either:
  - keep legacy county view and exclude post-switch `091xx` rows; or
  - show a stitched planning-region view with a visible methodology flag.

## 8. Why a weighted bridge is useful but not final

The bridge creates a practical way to test continuity, but the weight basis is a
methodology choice.

Current weight:

```text
PoUS tracked customers in the pilot town/city feature table
```

Possible alternatives:

- ACS population or households
- utility customers, if available
- land area
- outage exposure / historical outage counts
- address-level insured exposure, once quoting is live

PoUS tracked customers are useful because they match the pilot outage validation
sample. They are not necessarily the final exposure basis for all pricing.

## 9. Separate data-quality issue: 2019 gaps

The 2019 Northeast data gaps are separate from the CT county-equivalent
transition.

This note is about the 2025 CT FIPS geography switch. If a 2019 chunk is missing
across CT or broader Northeast counties, treat that as an EAGLE-I coverage or
ingestion gap and handle it in the data-quality audit, not as a CT planning-region
crosswalk problem.

## 10. Open decisions

Before promoting a CT stitch into pricing or trend metrics, decide:

1. Whether the canonical CT display should remain legacy-county keyed for v0 or
   move to planning-region keyed.
2. Which bridge weight basis is acceptable for actuarial review.
3. Whether stitched time series should be used for pricing, only diagnostics, or
   only dashboard continuity.
4. How to label CT rows in exports so users do not confuse observed raw source
   values with bridge-estimated values.
